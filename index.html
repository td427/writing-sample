Window dressing?

April retail sales + 34-year gas shock event study

April retail sales: +0.5% MoM, roughly in line with consensus. Control group: +0.5% (vs. 0.4% estimate). Gasoline stations: +2.8%, down sharply from March's +13.7%. That gap tells a different story.

Across 34 years of gas price data, I ran an event study on the four major geopolitical shock episodes — Katrina, '08 oil crisis, Arab Spring, Russia-Ukraine. The pattern holds in three of the four episodes: consumers absorb the shock and real retail recovers above baseline within 2–3 months. The exception is '08, but that episode was overwhelmed by the financial crisis hitting simultaneously, not the gas spike itself.

One number that makes this cycle harder to dismiss: monthly gas cost as a share of average weekly earnings is rising fast. Still beneath the '08 and '22 peaks, but the gap is gradually tightening.

Net Net The consumer is 3-for-3 against pure gas shocks. April's control group beat keeps that streak alive, but the discretionary cracks are already visible in the composition. History is in the bull's corner — the mix is not. 🧩

April CPI + QAFCO fertilizer shock → food lag thesis

April CPI: +3.8% YoY (vs. +3.7% expected). Energy mostly carried the headline again, but food at home rose +0.7% MoM — the biggest single-month move since the height of the 2022 inflation surge.

Here's why that +0.7% food print is worth paying attention to: QAFCO, a major Qatari fertilizer facility, went offline in early March after Iranian strikes disrupted its gas supply. It produces ~14% of global urea. Prices have surged ~81% since February. That cost increase is now sitting inside the input structure of crops being planted this spring — and it hasn't shown up in grocery prices yet.

Historical energy-to-food transmission lags about 5–6 months from the shock. The real food CPI inflection likely hits August–September, well after most are expecting the inflation picture to soften. On equities: domestic nitrogen producers stand to benefit directly; airlines are absorbing their own fuel-driven inflation without the stock upside.

Net Net The headline was hot, but the food lag makes it spicy. 🧩

All yield, no field.

S&P 500 capex concentration via Herfindahl-Hirschman Index

The S&P 500 is effectively two economies stitched together, and the seam is widening.

Applying the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index to estimated S&P 500 capex by sector, concentration has crossed well above the 2,500 "highly concentrated" threshold used in antitrust analysis — and is still climbing. Tech and AI drive an estimated 85% of that score in 2026E, up from ~24% in 2000 and ~23% a decade ago. The sharpest move came in a single year: 2023 to 2024.

Strip out the hyperscalers and non-AI corporate capex is running in the low single digits — energy, industrials, consumer sector weights in structural decline for a decade, per consensus.

The macro narrative is expansion. The HHI narrative is concentration. And the sectors losing ground are priced for a cycle even as the data hints at something longer.

Net Net The market calls it growth. The concentration index calls it something else. 🧩

Feel the beat.

April NFP: +115K vs. +60K consensus — reading the U-3/U-6 spread

April NFP: +115K. Consensus was +60K. A beat — but, as always, the internals tell a nuanced story.

Revisions: February revised down to −156K, March up to +185K. Net −16K combined — the 8:30 headline rarely sticks. AHE: +3.6% YoY, +0.2% MoM. 3-month annualized ~+3.2%, wages cooling on a short-run basis. Federal government shed another 9K, now down 348K from its October 2024 peak.

One number that warrants some attention: U-6 rose to 8.2% while U-3 held flat at 4.3% — the spread at its widest since late 2024. U-6 captures underemployment and involuntary part-time work, which jumped +445K to 4.9M in April. When U-3 is flat but U-6 is rising, people are finding work — just not the work they want.

On the Fed: rates on hold. Nothing in today's report changes that.

Net Net The headline clears the bar. The composition suggests the bar is getting lower. 🧩

Q1 earnings have been a reminder that "good" doesn't always move the needle.

S&P 500 beat-day reactions vs. miss penalties — consumer structural split

The S&P 500's average beat-day reaction has barely been positive, while misses still draw blood. The consumer sector's penalty is worse — even strong prints are met with selling.

Underneath the market reaction is the same ongoing consumer structural split. Premium cabins, steakhouses, and warehouse clubs continue to shine, while pizza chains and mid-tier retailers lose steam. The top end is still aspirational; the middle is defensive.

Sentiment data reinforces the divide. "Cautious consumer" mentions on earnings calls have surged, and confidence readings sit near multi-decade lows. Yet spending still hasn't collapsed — it's just selective. Experiences over essentials. Premium over practical. Intent over impulse.

It's a strange paradox: the economy is okay, the consumer is active, but confidence is cracked. The divergence is obvious, but the fatigue is louder.

Net Net As Miranda would say — that's all. 🧩

The small cap trade is working, but the balance sheet hasn't changed.

Russell 2000 floating-rate debt exposure, zombie companies & Fed rate regression

Russell 2000 is up ~12% YTD, outpacing the S&P 500 by a modest margin. The domestic revenue tilt is doing a lot of work. With multinationals absorbing geopolitical headwinds and a weaker USD, small caps are the relative safe haven — for now.

But: about 32% of R2K debt is floating rate vs. about 6% for the S&P 500. The Fed has held for three straight meetings. Small caps are staring down a $368B debt maturity wall in 2026 — refinancing at rates near 6.5% vs. the 1–2% many locked in a few years ago.

~41% of R2K constituents are "zombie" companies — EBIT can't cover interest expenses. That figure doesn't improve just because the index is outperforming. The regression going back to 1994 shows the directional relationship clearly: higher rates, tougher sledding for small caps relative to large.

Net Net Good time to be in small caps. Better time to know which ones. 🧩
